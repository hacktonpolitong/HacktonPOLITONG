# PilotOps AI — Product Specification Draft

## 1. Project Context

We are building a B2B AI-powered web application for the Shanghai International Hackathon 2026.

The product is designed for Chinese companies that already have a working warehouse automation product and want to enter the Italian market. The goal is not to produce a generic market report, but to help the company understand how to sell its first realistic pilot to an Italian warehouse/logistics buyer.

The product must be a working MVP, not only a presentation. AI must be the core engine of the workflow, not a decorative feature.

---

## 2. Working Product Name

Current working names:

- PilotOps AI
- Proof-to-Pilot
- Warehouse Pilot AI
- Pilot Entry Engine

Final name: **to be decided later**.

For now, we use **PilotOps AI** as the internal working name.

---

## 3. One-Line Definition

**PilotOps AI helps Chinese warehouse automation companies enter the Italian market by identifying the best first pilot opportunity: the right Italian buyer segment, the right warehouse process, the right trust gaps to close, the right pilot package to propose, and the first Italian target accounts to approach.**

---

## 4. Core Problem

Chinese warehouse automation companies may already have:

- a working product;
- competitive pricing;
- technical documentation;
- product specs;
- Chinese case studies;
- manufacturing capacity;
- sales ambition for Europe.

However, when entering Italy, they often do not know:

- which Italian customer segment is the best first target;
- which warehouse process should be automated first;
- what type of pilot an Italian buyer would realistically accept;
- what trust gaps block the buyer from saying yes;
- what documentation or proof must be shown;
- how to position the product against existing local or European alternatives;
- how to communicate the offer in a credible, low-risk way.
- which concrete Italian companies are most compatible with the recommended pilot.

The main problem is therefore not simply “market entry”.

The real problem is:

> **How can a Chinese warehouse automation company sell its first pilot to an Italian buyer that has never heard of it before?**

---

## 5. Product Thesis

A market report is not enough.

A traditional report may explain market size, competitors, macro trends and regulatory conditions. However, it does not directly answer the operational sales question:

> **Who should we approach, why now, what should we propose, what proof do they need, what pilot can they approve with low risk, and which Italian companies should we start from?**

PilotOps AI is designed to turn product information and documentation into a practical **Pilot Entry Package**.

The output should make the Head of Expansion feel:

> “Now I know what to do next Monday morning.”

---

## 6. Buyer of Our Product

The buyer/user of PilotOps AI is:

> **Head of International Expansion of a Chinese warehouse automation company.**

Secondary possible users:

- CEO / founder;
- international sales manager;
- GTM manager;
- business development manager;
- strategy / market entry team.

The buyer is not the Italian warehouse company.

The Italian warehouse company is the final customer that the Chinese company wants to win.

## 7. Client Company Profile

The ideal Chinese client company is any company that sells products or solutions related to warehouse automation, including:

- AMR robots;
- AGV systems;
- robotic picking systems;
- sorting automation;
- palletizing automation;
- robotic arms for warehouse operations;
- inventory scanning robots;
- WMS / AI orchestration software;
- warehouse process optimization tools;
- integrated automation solutions for logistics and fulfilment.

The company is assumed to already have:

- product documentation in Chinese or English;
- product specs;
- technical material;
- a working product;
- a basic understanding of its product category;
- interest in entering the Italian market.

## 8. Target Market

The target market for the MVP is:

> **Italy — warehouse automation and logistics operations.**

This includes Italian companies operating in:

- third-party logistics;
- e-commerce fulfilment;
- retail logistics;
- industrial warehouses;
- manufacturing warehouses;
- food and beverage logistics;
- pharma logistics;
- parcel and sorting operations;
- distribution centres.

The MVP does not try to cover all European markets.

The focus is specifically:

> **Chinese warehouse automation companies → Italian warehouse/logistics market → first pilot sale.**

## 9. Final Customer Selection Logic

The final Italian customer is not fixed in advance.

Instead, the AI should select the most suitable customer segment based on the product type.

Examples:

| Chinese Product Type | Likely Italian Buyer Segment |
|---|---|
| AMR / AGV | 3PL warehouses, e-commerce fulfilment, retail logistics |
| Sorting automation | parcel logistics, fulfilment centres, courier networks |
| Picking robot | e-commerce, pharma logistics, spare parts warehouses |
| Palletizing automation | food & beverage, manufacturing warehouses, industrial distribution |
| WMS / AI orchestration | structured logistics operators, multi-site warehouses |
| Inventory scanning robot | retail warehouses, fashion logistics, spare parts logistics |

This is a key product principle:

> **Given the product, the system identifies the most suitable Italian pilot buyer.**

## 10. Main Product Promise

PilotOps AI should combine the first three promises below:

1. **Tell the company where to enter the Italian warehouse market.**
2. **Tell the company who to sell to first, including a short list of compatible Italian companies.**
3. **Build the most realistic first pilot package.**
4. Avoid becoming only a generic market report generator.

The strongest positioning is:

> **We do not generate market reports. We generate first-pilot entry strategies.**

Alternative pitch sentence:

> **A market report tells you where the market is. PilotOps AI tells you where an Italian buyer is ready to say yes to a pilot.**

## 11. MVP Scope

The MVP must be narrow, credible and demoable.

### Included in the MVP

The MVP should include:

- product intake form;
- documentation readiness checklist;
- AI analysis engine;
- Italian customer segment recommendation;
- warehouse process recommendation;
- trust gap analysis;
- pilot offer generation;
- buyer objection simulation;
- Target Account Shortlist with relevant Italian companies from a curated target-account database;
- sales pack generation;
- final dashboard called **Pilot Control Room**.

### Excluded from the MVP

The MVP should not attempt to:

- cover all European countries;
- perform full legal or compliance certification;
- guarantee real sales leads;
- build a full CRM;
- scrape the entire web at scale;
- collect private personal contact data or personal decision-maker emails;
- create a generic consulting report;
- behave like a simple chatbot;
- promise that the pilot will certainly be sold.

## 13. Input Requirements

The MVP input should be simple.

The user provides:

- product description;
- product category;
- Chinese documentation or a summary of documentation;
- current proof available;
- target market: Italy.

The MVP should not require perfect documentation at the start.

Instead, it should say:

> **Based on what you provided, here is your current readiness level. To increase buyer trust, you should prepare these missing proofs.**

This makes the system useful even if documentation is incomplete.

## 14. AI Core Functions

AI must be central to the product.

The AI should perform the following functions:

### 14.1 Product Understanding

The AI reads the product description and classifies:

- product type;
- warehouse use case;
- operational value;
- likely installation complexity;
- likely buyer concerns;
- suitable pilot scale.

### 14.2 Product-to-Buyer Matching

The AI matches the product with the most suitable Italian buyer segment.

Example:

- AMR → 3PL / e-commerce fulfilment;
- sorting system → parcel logistics;
- palletizing automation → food & beverage / manufacturing;
- inventory scanning robot → retail / fashion logistics.

### 14.3 Warehouse Process Selection

The AI identifies which warehouse process should be automated first.

Possible process options:

- inbound handling;
- internal transport;
- picking support;
- packing flow;
- sorting;
- pallet movement;
- inventory scanning;
- night-shift automation;
- loading/unloading;
- dispatch preparation.

This is one of the most important engineering elements of the product.

The system should not simply say “sell the robot”.

It should say:

> **Deploy the robot in this specific workflow, with this scope, these constraints and these KPIs.**

### 14.4 Trust Gap Analysis

The AI identifies what prevents an Italian buyer from saying yes.

Possible trust gaps:

- no Italian case study;
- no European reference;
- unclear maintenance model;
- unclear CE / safety documentation;
- unclear WMS integration;
- unclear installation time;
- uncertain ROI;
- no local partner;
- fear of operational disruption;
- concern about language and support;
- concern about spare parts availability.

### 14.5 Buyer Objection Simulation

The AI simulates the likely objections of an Italian buyer.

Example objections:

- “Who maintains the system locally?”
- “How does this integrate with our WMS?”
- “What happens if the robot fails during operations?”
- “Do you have CE and safety documentation?”
- “Why should we choose you instead of an Italian or European provider?”
- “How can I justify this internally?”
- “What is the ROI in our warehouse conditions?”

### 14.6 Pilot Offer Generation

The AI generates a realistic first pilot.

Pilot structure should include:

- target buyer segment;
- warehouse process;
- pilot duration;
- pilot scope;
- number of robots/systems;
- required setup;
- KPIs;
- data to collect;
- buyer risk reduction;
- exit clause;
- proof required before launch;
- next commercial step.

### 14.7 Sales Pack Generation

The AI generates materials that can be used immediately:

- first outreach email;
- pilot one-pager;
- buyer objection battlecard;
- short pitch for first call;
- ROI argument;
- proof checklist;
- next-step plan.

### 14.8 Target Account Shortlist Generation

The AI generates and ranks a short list of Italian companies that are compatible with the recommended buyer segment and pilot process.

This section must use a curated target-account database, not broad live web scraping. The planned MVP dataset is `data/italian_target_accounts.json`: a local, controlled list of Italian companies classified by segment, warehouse process fit, geography, company-level public contact information, and source notes.

The shortlist should include:

- company name;
- official website;
- region and city;
- matched buyer segment;
- matched warehouse process;
- fit score;
- reason why the company is relevant;
- recommended buyer roles, such as Operations Director, Warehouse Manager, or Head of Contract Logistics;
- public contact page or generic company email when available;
- personalized outreach angle.

The system should prefer company-level public contact details and role-based outreach. It must not promise private personal contacts, personal decision-maker emails, LinkedIn scraping, mass personal-data collection, guaranteed replies, or guaranteed buyers.

## 15. Pilot Control Room — Expected Output

The final output should not feel like a long report.

It should be a dashboard that creates clarity and momentum.

Example structure:

### 15.1 Best First Buyer

Example:

> **Mid-size Italian 3PL warehouses handling e-commerce and retail fulfilment in Northern Italy.**

Reasoning:

- enough operational complexity to need automation;
- not as slow as large enterprise procurement;
- likely pressure on cost and efficiency;
- suitable for limited-scope pilots.

### 15.2 Best Pilot Use Case

Example:

> **Autonomous internal transport between picking and packing areas.**

The system should explain:

- why this workflow is suitable;
- why it is low-risk;
- why it is measurable;
- why it can be approved as a pilot.

### 15.3 Why Now

The system should explain the timing logic.

Example signals:

- pressure to improve warehouse productivity;
- labour cost pressure;
- need for automation without full redesign;
- growth of fulfilment operations;
- increasing interest in robotics and AI-driven logistics.

### 15.4 Trust Gap Analysis

Example table:

| Trust Gap | Risk Level | Recommended Fix |
|---|---:|---|
| No Italian case study | High | Start with a limited pilot with measurable KPIs |
| Local maintenance unclear | High | Present support SLA and local partner plan |
| ROI not localized | Medium | Build ROI using Italian labour/process assumptions |
| Compliance summary unclear | Medium | Prepare one-page technical and safety summary |
| Integration with WMS unclear | Medium | Provide integration workflow and API/data requirements |

### 15.5 Recommended Pilot Offer

Example:

> **45-day pilot in one warehouse zone**  
> Scope: automate transport between picking and packing  
> Setup: 2 AMRs, 1 mapped route, no full warehouse redesign  
> KPI: reduce manual walking time by 15–25%  
> Risk reducer: exit clause after pilot  
> Required proof: technical specs, safety summary, support plan, integration checklist

### 15.6 Buyer Objection Battlecard

Example:

| Buyer Objection | Suggested Response |
|---|---|
| “We do not know your brand.” | Start with a limited pilot, measurable KPIs and exit clause. |
| “What if maintenance is slow?” | Provide SLA, spare parts plan and remote support process. |
| “Will this disrupt operations?” | Deploy in one controlled workflow, not the full warehouse. |
| “How do we justify ROI?” | Measure labour time saved, throughput improvement and error reduction. |

### 15.7 Sales Pack

The system should generate:

- first outreach email;
- meeting pitch;
- one-page pilot proposal;
- proof checklist;
- objection battlecard;
- short action plan.

### 15.8 Target Account Shortlist

The system should show 5-10 Italian companies that are highly compatible with the recommended buyer segment and pilot process.

Example for an AMR internal transport product:

> If the recommended segment is mid-size 3PL / e-commerce fulfilment and the recommended process is internal transport between picking and packing, the shortlist should prioritize compatible Italian 3PL or e-commerce fulfilment companies, especially in Lombardy, Veneto, Emilia-Romagna, or Piedmont.

Each account should include:

- company name;
- website;
- region and city;
- matched buyer segment;
- matched warehouse process;
- fit score;
- reason why this company is relevant;
- recommended buyer roles;
- public contact page or generic company email when available;
- personalized outreach angle;
- confidence level and source notes.

The shortlist is not exhaustive and should not be presented as a guaranteed lead list. If account confidence is low, the dashboard should show `needs verification`.

## 16. Data Strategy

The MVP should use a mix of real and simulated data.

Goal:

> Use as much real evidence as possible while keeping the MVP feasible.

### 16.1 Company Data

Provided by the Chinese company:

- product description;
- product documentation;
- product specs;
- Chinese case studies;
- technical advantages;
- certifications or readiness statements;
- pricing range, if available;
- support model, if available.

### 16.2 Market / Segment Data

For the MVP, we can seed the system with curated data about Italian warehouse/logistics segments:

- 3PL;
- e-commerce fulfilment;
- retail logistics;
- manufacturing warehouses;
- food & beverage logistics;
- pharma logistics;
- parcel sorting;
- industrial distribution.

### 16.3 Competitor / Alternative Data

The system can include a small curated dataset of typical alternatives:

- local integrators;
- European automation providers;
- manual labour;
- traditional conveyors;
- existing WMS/process improvements;
- large enterprise automation solutions.

### 16.4 Buyer Objection Dataset

Seed a dataset of common buyer objections for Italian warehouse automation buyers:

- maintenance;
- support;
- integration;
- safety;
- ROI;
- installation downtime;
- lack of local references;
- procurement risk;
- language and communication;
- spare parts.

### 16.5 Documentation Checklist Dataset

Seed a dataset of common proof requirements:

- product specs;
- safety documentation;
- CE-related documentation summary;
- installation plan;
- maintenance plan;
- support SLA;
- integration requirements;
- ROI model;
- case study;
- pilot proposal;
- warranty terms.

### 16.6 Curated Target-Account Dataset

The MVP should include a planned curated target-account database:

## 17. Recommended MVP Technical Architecture

### Frontend

Recommended stack:

- React / Next.js;
- Tailwind CSS;
- card-based dashboard;
- animated analysis flow;
- export/copy buttons for generated materials.

### Backend

Recommended stack:

- Node.js / Next.js API routes or Express;
- structured prompt pipeline;
- JSON schema outputs;
- seeded dataset in JSON files;
- optional document text ingestion.

### AI Layer

The AI should be called through structured steps, not one generic prompt.

Suggested pipeline:

1. Parse product profile.
2. Classify product category.
3. Match product to Italian warehouse segment.
4. Select best pilot process.
5. Find and rank compatible target accounts from the curated target-account database.
6. Identify trust gaps.
7. Generate pilot offer.
8. Generate sales pack.
9. Return structured JSON for dashboard rendering.

### Data Layer

For MVP:

- local JSON seed files;
- optionally a few curated source snippets;
- demo product profile;
- planned curated target-account database for Italian company shortlisting;
- no need for a complex database.
